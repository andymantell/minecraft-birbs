# Barn Owl (Tyto alba) -- Detailed Reference for Minecraft Mod

---

## Physical Appearance

### Body Dimensions

| Measurement | Male | Female | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Body length** | 32--38 cm | 33--40 cm | Females slightly longer |
| **Wingspan** | 85--93 cm | 85--98 cm | Some sources cite up to 110 cm tip-to-tip |
| **Weight** | ~330 g (range 240--360 g) | ~360 g (range 280--480 g) | Females ~10--20% heavier; female weight peaks before egg-laying (up to 425 g) |
| **Standing height** | ~34 cm | ~34 cm | Appears taller than body length due to upright perch posture |

The Barn Owl is a medium-sized owl -- roughly pigeon-sized in body but appearing much larger due to its broad wings and long legs.

### Detailed Plumage Description (British subspecies T. a. alba)

**Facial disc:**
Heart-shaped, pure white to creamy white. Rimmed with a narrow border of buff-brown to tawny feathers (the "ruff"). The disc functions as a parabolic sound collector directing sound to the asymmetrically placed ears. Females may show a slightly darker brown rim around the facial disc than males.

**Eyes:**
Dark, blackish-brown (almost black). This distinguishes the Barn Owl from most other owl species which have yellow or orange eyes.

**Bill:**
Pale horn to pinkish-buff, relatively small and hooked downward. Colour corresponds with overall plumage tone.

**Crown and nape:**
Golden-buff to tawny-orange, finely peppered with small grey and white flecks. Scattered tiny blackish spots.

**Mantle and back (upperparts):**
Rich golden-buff to tawny-orange, delicately vermiculated (finely patterned) with pale grey and white. Small dark spots (blackish-brown) are scattered across the dorsal feathers, each spot positioned centrally near the feather tip.

**Rump:**
Similar to the back -- golden-buff with grey vermiculations and dark spotting, but can appear slightly paler.

**Tail:**
Short relative to body. Golden-buff with typically 4--5 narrow dark bars across the feathers.

**Upperwing:**
Golden-buff to tawny-orange across the coverts, blending to slightly paler on the flight feathers. Primaries and secondaries are light brown/buff with darker barring. Leading edge of flight feathers has tiny comb-like serrations (key adaptation for silent flight).

**Underwing:**
Predominantly white to pale buff. Flight feathers show faint barring visible from below. The underwing appears very pale and ghostly in flight, especially at dusk.

**Breast:**
Pure white (T. a. alba, the British/western European subspecies). Clean and unmarked in males; females may show a faint buff wash and sparse small dark speckles.

**Belly:**
White, sometimes with a buff wash. Females typically display more dark flecking than males.

**Flanks:**
White to buff. Females characteristically show small blackish-brown spots/flecks on the flanks, which may extend across the chest. Males have cleaner, more purely white flanks with few or no spots.

**Undertail coverts:**
White, generally unmarked.

**Legs and feet:**
Long, sparsely feathered with short whitish bristle-like feathers. Toes bare and greyish-pink. Talons dark grey to blackish, sharp and curved.

### Sexual Dimorphism

- Females are **larger and heavier** (~10--20% heavier) than males.
- Females are generally **darker overall** -- richer buff tones above, more buff wash below.
- Females display **more and larger black spots** on the breast, belly, and particularly the flanks. Males are purer white underneath.
- Female **throat** tends to be buff-tinted; male throat is whiter.
- Female **facial disc rim** can be slightly darker brown than the male's.
- About 90% of UK Barn Owls can be sexed visually by plumage.

### Juvenile Plumage

- **Natal down (0--6 days):** Short, dense, pure white down. Sparse on the central belly. The heart-shaped facial disc outline becomes visible soon after hatching.
- **Second down (6+ days):** White down is gradually replaced by longer, buff-coloured ("mesoptile") down, giving a fluffy cream appearance.
- **2 weeks old:** About half adult weight. Appear somewhat naked as downy covering is insufficient for their growing bodies.
- **3 weeks old:** Quill pins start pushing through the skin. Chicks stand upright and beg with wings raised.
- **8--9 weeks:** Fully feathered; juvenile plumage closely resembles adult plumage. Juvenile feathers may appear slightly fresher and more uniformly coloured than worn adult feathers, but differences are subtle.
- Juvenile plumage does **not** show strongly distinct patterning from adults once down is fully replaced.

## Shape and Proportions

### Body Shape
- **Facial disc:** The defining feature -- a broad, flat, heart-shaped face unique among owls. No ear tufts.
- **Body:** Relatively slender and elongated compared to Tawny or Little Owls. Medium-length torso.
- **Legs:** Notably long for an owl (an adaptation for striking into long grass). Legs extend well below the body when perched.
- **Wings:** Broad and rounded, large relative to body mass. This gives a low wing-loading, enabling slow, buoyant flight.
- **Tail:** Relatively short and square-ended.
- **Head:** Large and rounded, with no ear tufts. The head can rotate ~270 degrees.

### Typical Postures

**Perched:**
Upright posture with a forward-leaning body. The long legs are clearly visible. Often stands on one leg when roosting. Head is held high and the facial disc faces directly forward. When alarmed, elongates the body vertically and sways side to side. Overall silhouette is tall and slim compared to the rounder, squatter Tawny Owl.

**In flight:**
Wings held slightly forward and below the horizontal -- not flat like a harrier but more angled. Legs trail behind or dangle when hovering or about to strike. The overall impression is of a large, pale, ghostly bird with a buoyant, wavering flight. Head appears large relative to body. Upperwing shows golden-buff; underwing and underparts appear brilliantly white, especially in low light or torchlight.

## Habitat and Biome

### Preferred Habitats in Britain
- **Lowland farmland** with a mixture of arable fields and **rough grassland**. This is the core habitat.
- **Rough grassland margins** along field edges, hedgerows, ditches, river banks, and road verges. Rough grassland with a tussocky structure and a thick litter layer (at least 7 cm deep) supports the highest densities of Field Voles, their primary prey.
- **River valleys and flood meadows** -- particularly favoured.
- **Coastal marshes and grazing marshes.**
- **Village edges and farmsteads** -- traditionally strongly associated with old agricultural buildings (barns, church towers, ruins).
- They **avoid** dense woodland, moorland, uplands, and urban centres.
- Range: Throughout lowland England and Wales; scarce in Scotland; present in Ireland.

### Minecraft Biome Mapping

| Real Habitat | Suggested Minecraft Biome |
|---|---|
| Lowland farmland / rough grassland | **Plains**, **Sunflower Plains** |
| River valleys, flood meadows | **River** biome edges, **Swamp** edges |
| Coastal marshes | **Swamp**, **Mangrove Swamp** (loosely) |
| Village edges / farm buildings | **Village** structures, **Plains Village** |
| Avoiding dense forest | Should NOT spawn in **Dark Forest**, **Old Growth** forests, **Taigas** |
| Avoiding uplands/mountains | Should NOT spawn in **Mountains**, **Windswept** biomes |

Best primary spawn biome: **Plains** (and variants), near **Village** structures.

### Roosting and Nesting Sites
- Old barns, farm buildings, church towers, ruins, derelict buildings.
- Large hollow trees (particularly old oaks and ash).
- Purpose-built nest boxes (readily adopted).
- Modern farm buildings with owl access holes.
- Barn Owls show **strong site fidelity** -- once settled, they use the same roost and nest sites for life.
- During non-breeding season, males and females may roost separately at different sites within their home range.

### Relationship with Farmland and Buildings
- One of the most farmland-associated raptors in Britain.
- Historically nested almost exclusively in agricultural buildings (hence the name "Barn" Owl).
- Loss of old barns, conversion of farm buildings, and intensification of farming (loss of rough grassland) are major threats.
- The Barn Owl Trust and conservation bodies have installed thousands of nest boxes to compensate for lost natural sites.
- Requires 14--47 hectares of rough grassland within 2 km of a nest site, depending on landscape type.

## Diet and Feeding

### Primary Prey

In Britain, the diet comprises approximately:

| Prey Species | Approximate % of Diet |
|---|---|
| **Field Vole** (Microtus agrestis) | ~50--60% (dominant prey) |
| **Common Shrew** (Sorex araneus) | ~15--20% |
| **Wood Mouse** (Apodelis sylvaticus) | ~10--15% |
| **Pygmy Shrew** (Sorex minutus) | ~2--5% |
| **Brown Rat** (Rattus norvegicus) | Occasional |
| **Bank Vole** (Myodes glareolus) | Occasional |
| **Harvest Mouse** (Micromys minutus) | Rare |
| **Small birds** (sparrows, starlings) | Occasional (~5%) |
| **Frogs and insects** | Rare |

Together, Field Voles, Common Shrews, and Wood Mice make up approximately **82%** of Barn Owl diet in the UK.

Barn Owls show no specific food preferences beyond a strong disinterest in invertebrates. They are opportunistic, taking whatever small vertebrates are available and catchable.

### Hunting Technique

1. **Quartering flight:** The primary hunting method. The owl flies slowly and deliberately back and forth over rough grassland at **1--3 metres height**, listening for prey below. Flight is slow (as low as 2 mph / 3 km/h), buoyant, and wavering.
2. **Hovering:** When a sound is detected, the owl pauses to hover in the air above the suspected prey location, pinpointing it precisely.
3. **Strike:** The owl plunges feet-first into vegetation, grabbing the prey with its talons. It may also land near where it heard a sound and wait for additional noise before leaping and striking.
4. **Perch-and-pounce:** Less common. The owl perches on a fence post, tree branch, or similar vantage point, scanning/listening for prey, then swoops down to catch it.
5. **Hearing-based location:** The heart-shaped facial disc funnels sound to asymmetrically positioned ears. The tiny time difference between sound reaching the left and right ears enables precise three-dimensional location of prey -- even in complete darkness.

Prey is **swallowed whole** (head-first for larger items).

### Pellet Production
- Barn Owls regurgitate **1--2 pellets per day** (typically after a night's hunting, often at the roost site).
- Each pellet is roughly **3--7 cm long**, smooth, rounded, and blackish when fresh (becoming grey and crumbly with age).
- Each pellet typically contains remains of **4--5 small mammals** (fur, bones, skulls, teeth).
- Pellets accumulate below regular roost and nest sites in large quantities over time, forming a compacted layer.

### Seasonal Diet Changes
- Diet is dominated by **Field Voles** year-round, but proportions shift with vole population cycles (which peak roughly every 3--4 years).
- In **winter**, when voles are scarcer, the proportion of shrews and wood mice in the diet increases.
- In **harsh winters**, Barn Owls may struggle to find sufficient prey, leading to significant mortality (they have very low fat reserves).
- In years of vole abundance ("vole plague years"), breeding success and double-brooding rates increase.

## Flight Pattern

### Flight Style
- **Silent:** Barn Owl flight is essentially noiseless, achieved through three feather adaptations:
  1. Comb-like serrations on the leading edge of flight feathers break up turbulent air flow.
  2. A soft, velvety dorsal surface texture on flight feathers absorbs sound.
  3. A fine hair-like fringe on the trailing edge of flight feathers smooths the air leaving the wing.
- **Buoyant and wavering:** Due to low wing-loading (large wing area relative to light body mass), the flight appears light, floating, and somewhat moth-like.
- **Slow:** Can fly as slowly as 2 mph (3 km/h) while hunting. Normal flight speed is approximately 10--20 mph.
- **Ghostly:** The white underparts and underwings catch available light, making the bird appear luminous and ghost-like at dusk and dawn. This may be the origin of some "ghost" legends.

### Wing Beat Pattern
- Slow, deep, deliberate wing beats interspersed with long glides.
- Wings are held slightly below the horizontal during glides (not flat like a harrier).
- Wing beats are fluid and loose, not stiff or rapid.
- When quartering, the flight alternates between a few flaps and extended glides.

### Typical Flight Height
- **Hunting:** 1--3 metres above the ground. This low flight makes them vulnerable to collisions with vehicles on roads.
- **Commuting:** Somewhat higher, but rarely more than 10--15 metres above ground.
- Generally a low-flying bird at all times.

### Hovering Behaviour
- Barn Owls **hover frequently** when hunting -- pausing in mid-air above a spot where they have heard prey.
- Hovering bouts are typically brief (a few seconds up to ~20 seconds).
- During hovering, the legs dangle below the body, ready to strike.
- The head remains remarkably stable during hovering while the body adjusts.

### Differences from Other Owls
- Much paler and more ghostly than Tawny Owl (which is brown, stocky, and flies with stiffer, more direct wing beats).
- Longer-winged and more buoyant than Little Owl (which has a bounding, woodpecker-like flight).
- Lacks the ear tufts visible in flight of Long-eared Owl.
- Shares quartering hunting flight with Short-eared Owl, but Barn Owl is paler, slimmer, and has a distinctive heart-shaped face visible even in flight.

## Nesting Behaviour

### Nest Site Preferences
1. **Old barns and farm buildings** -- the traditional and namesake nest site. Dark, enclosed spaces with access holes.
2. **Tree hollows** -- large cavities in mature oaks, ash, and other broadleaved trees.
3. **Purpose-built nest boxes** -- widely adopted; placed in buildings, on trees, or on poles. Barn Owls readily use artificial nest boxes.
4. **Church towers, ruins, old stone buildings.**
5. **Cliff crevices and rock faces** (rare in Britain).
6. Sites must be **enclosed and dark**, with a flat surface for eggs.

### Nest Construction
- **Barn Owls do NOT build a nest.** They lay eggs directly onto the bare surface -- whether that is a wooden floor, stone ledge, or accumulated old pellet debris.
- The female may scrape a shallow depression in existing debris and break up some old pellets to create a softer substrate.
- Over successive years, a thick layer of compacted pellet material, feathers, and prey remains accumulates at the nest site.

### Breeding Season
- **Egg-laying:** Typically begins in **mid-April to early May** in the UK. Average first egg date has shifted from May 9th (pre-1990) to approximately April 17th in recent decades (attributed to climate change).
- Nesting has been recorded in every month of the year, but most pairs breed only in spring.
- Laying can extend into summer for late breeders or second broods.

### Clutch Size and Incubation
- **Clutch size:** 4--7 eggs, typically 4--6. Average ~4.7--4.9 eggs. Earlier clutches tend to be larger.
- **Eggs:** White, slightly glossy, oval-shaped, relatively small (~40 x 30 mm).
- **Laying interval:** One egg every 2--3 days.
- **Incubation:** Begins with the **first egg** (asynchronous hatching -- chicks hatch in sequence, so the oldest chick can be 2+ weeks older than the youngest).
- **Incubation period:** 31--32 days per egg.
- **Incubation duty:** Female alone incubates. The male provides all food to the female throughout incubation and early brooding.
- **Brooding:** Female broods chicks continuously until the oldest is about 3 weeks old. Total time on the nest for the female (incubation + brooding) is approximately 9 weeks.

### Fledging and Independence
- **First flights:** 8--9 weeks (50--55 days) after hatching.
- **Competent flyers:** By 10 weeks.
- **Post-fledging dependence:** Young return to the nest to roost and receive food from parents for 4--6 weeks after fledging.
- **First prey capture:** Earliest recorded at 72 days old (about 10 weeks).
- **Independence:** Owlets begin dispersing at ~13 weeks old, with most having left the parents' home range by 14 weeks.

### Broods Per Year
- Usually **one brood per year**.
- In good vole years, approximately **10% of UK pairs** may raise a **second brood** (double-brooding).
- Double-brooding requires ample food supply for at least 6 months (April--September) and sufficiently early start to the first brood.
- Barn Owls and Short-eared Owls are the only UK owl species that regularly double-brood.

## Song and Calls

Barn Owls do **not hoot**. Their vocalisations are entirely different from the typical owl "hoot" (which is the Tawny Owl).

### Call Types

**Advertising call / Screech (male):**
A long, drawn-out, eerie shrieking screech lasting 1--2 seconds. Raspy, wavering, and somewhat metallic. Used by males in flight to advertise territory and attract a mate. This is the iconic Barn Owl sound and the source of the folk name "Screech Owl." Pitch varies but is generally high.

**Distress call:**
A series of prolonged, harsh screams -- louder and more urgent than the advertising call. Given when alarmed or under threat.

**Warning call:**
A sharp, high-pitched scream or shriek. Given near the nest when disturbed.

**Purring call (male):**
A quiet, trembling screech or chittering purr used by males to entice females to a nest site.

**Hissing:**
A loud, prolonged hiss (like steam escaping), lasting several seconds and repeated. Given by both adults and chicks as a defensive threat display -- directed at intruders or predators approaching the nest. Accompanied by bill-snapping.

**Bill-snapping / clicking:**
Rapid clicking of the bill as a threat display, often combined with hissing and swaying.

**Chirrups and twitters:**
Soft, conversational sounds exchanged between pair members. Males chirrup when delivering food; females chirrup when feeding young.

**Snoring (begging call -- chicks):**
Young chicks produce a rasping, wheezy "snoring" call when begging for food, with wings raised and tail waggling. This call is persistent and audible from some distance.

### When They Call
- Mainly at **dusk and during the night**, particularly during the breeding season (March--August).
- Males screech most actively during courtship and territory establishment.
- Hissing and bill-snapping occur when the nest is disturbed at any time.
- Chick begging calls occur throughout the night during the breeding season.

## Behaviour

### Nocturnal Activity Patterns
- **Primarily nocturnal.** Most hunting activity occurs between dusk and dawn.
- Becomes active **shortly before dusk**, sometimes visible hunting in late afternoon, especially in winter when nights are long and food is scarce.
- **Crepuscular hunting** (dawn and dusk) is common, and daytime hunting occurs when food demands are high (e.g., when feeding chicks, or in winter).
- Median nightly active period: approximately **6.8 hours**, with variation from about 5 minutes to 10+ hours depending on conditions and season.
- During active periods: approximately **78% of time perching**, **13% hunting**, **10% commuting** between sites.

### Territorial Behaviour
- Barn Owls are **loosely territorial**. They establish a home range rather than a strictly defended territory.
- Home range size varies: typically **1--5 km radius** around the nest site, depending on prey availability.
- Males patrol the home range, screeching in flight to advertise their presence, particularly during courtship.
- Single males establish feeding territories and screech from prominent perches to attract mates.
- Home ranges of neighbouring pairs may overlap at the edges.
- Outside the breeding season, pairs may roost apart but generally remain within the same home range.

### Hunting Behaviour (Detailed)
- Relies primarily on **hearing** rather than sight to locate prey, although vision is also excellent in low light.
- The heart-shaped facial disc acts as a **parabolic reflector**, channelling sound to the ears.
- Ears are **asymmetrically positioned** on the skull (left ear higher than right), enabling precise vertical as well as horizontal sound localisation.
- Can locate and catch prey in **total darkness** using hearing alone.
- Hunts by quartering back and forth over grassland at 1--3 m height, pausing to hover when prey is detected, then plunging feet-first.
- May also perch-hunt from fence posts and branches, dropping onto prey detected by sound.
- When prey is heard from a perch, the owl may fly to the vicinity, land, listen for further sound, then leap and strike from a short distance.

### Roosting Behaviour
- Roosts during the day in **dark, sheltered sites**: barns, buildings, tree hollows, dense ivy, nest boxes.
- Roosts almost exclusively in **buildings** during daytime.
- At night, between hunting bouts, perches in **pastures, meadows, and forest edges**.
- Shows **strong roost site fidelity** -- uses the same roost sites habitually.
- Multiple roost sites may be used within the home range, with different sites for different purposes or weather conditions.
- During breeding season, the female roosts at the nest site; the male roosts separately nearby.

### Pellet Casting
- Pellets are regurgitated approximately **twice per day**, typically at the roost or nest site.
- Pellet casting usually occurs after returning from a night's hunting, before settling to roost.
- The process involves the owl sitting still, then stretching its neck and opening its bill wide to disgorge the pellet.
- Pellets accumulate in large quantities below favoured roosts, sometimes forming mounds several centimetres deep over years.
- Pellet analysis is a primary method for scientists to study Barn Owl diet and local small mammal populations.
