# Mallard (Anas platyrhynchos) -- Detailed Reference for Minecraft Mod

> The most familiar and widespread dabbling duck in the world. The ancestor of nearly all domestic duck breeds (except Muscovy). Often the standard against which all other ducks are compared.

---

## 1. Physical Appearance

### 1.1 Body Dimensions

| Measurement | Value |
|---|---|
| **Total length** | 50--65 cm (20--26 in); body makes up roughly two-thirds |
| **Wingspan** | 81--98 cm (32--39 in) |
| **Weight** | 0.7--1.6 kg (1.5--3.5 lb) |
| **Bill length** | 4.4--6.1 cm (1.7--2.4 in) |
| **Nest diameter** | ~30 cm (1 ft) |

Males are generally at the larger end of these ranges, females at the smaller end.

---

### 1.2 Male Breeding Plumage (Alternate Plumage)

The drake in full breeding condition is one of the most recognisable birds in the world.

| Body Part | Colour / Description |
|---|---|
| **Head & upper neck** | Glossy, iridescent bottle-green (can flash purple/teal depending on light angle). Smooth, rounded profile. |
| **Eye** | Dark brown iris. |
| **Bill** | Bright yellow to yellowish-orange, with a black nail (tip). |
| **Neck ring** | Narrow but distinct white collar separating the green head from the breast. The ring is incomplete at the back of the neck. |
| **Breast / chest** | Rich chestnut / purple-tinged maroon-brown. Sharply demarcated from the grey flanks below. |
| **Flanks & belly** | Pale silvery-grey with very fine vermiculations (wavy pencil-thin lines visible close up). |
| **Back** | Grey-brown. |
| **Rump & undertail coverts** | Black. Forms a distinctive black triangle visible from below in flight. |
| **Tail** | Central tail feathers black and **curled upward** (the famous "drake curl" or "sex curl" -- a key identifying feature). Outer tail feathers white/pale grey. |
| **Upperwing coverts** | Grey-brown. |
| **Speculum** | Iridescent purple-blue (see section 1.6 below). |
| **Underwing** | White / pale silvery. |
| **Legs & feet** | Bright orange-red with dark webbing. |

---

### 1.3 Female Plumage

The hen is cryptically coloured for camouflage while nesting.

| Body Part | Colour / Description |
|---|---|
| **Crown** | Dark brown, darker than the rest of the head. |
| **Eyestripe** | Dark brown stripe running through the eye from bill to nape. |
| **Supercilium (eyebrow)** | Buff / pale, contrasting with the dark crown and eyestripe. |
| **Cheeks & throat** | Buff to pale cream. |
| **Neck** | Buff, streaked with brown. |
| **Breast & flanks** | Mottled brown -- each individual feather has a dark brown centre with paler buff margins, giving a scalloped or "V"-shaped pattern. Side and flank colours are variable: reddish-brown, tawny, or creamy. |
| **Belly** | Variable -- can be pale buff, lightly streaked, or spotted with brown. |
| **Back** | Dark brown with buff feather edges. |
| **Tail** | Whitish outer tail feathers; central feathers brown. |
| **Speculum** | Same iridescent purple-blue with white borders as the male (key identification feature shared by both sexes). |
| **Bill** | Dark -- ranges from black to mottled orange-and-brown. Often has an orange base with dark saddle/ridge. |
| **Eye** | Dark brown. |
| **Legs & feet** | Orange (duller than the male's, but still clearly orange). |

---

### 1.4 Eclipse Plumage (Male, Jun--Sep)

After the breeding season (June--August), drakes moult into **eclipse plumage** -- a drab, female-like appearance that provides camouflage during the flightless moult period.

- Overall appearance: streaky, mottled brown, closely resembling the female.
- **Key differences from female in eclipse:**
  - Breast tends to be warmer/richer brown than the female's.
  - Plumage often looks messier / less neatly patterned than the female's.
  - Traces of green remain on the head -- typically green stripes on the crown and behind the eye.
  - Bill remains yellow/yellowish (female's bill is darker/more mottled).
- By early September, males begin moulting back into breeding plumage. Full breeding plumage is typically regained by October--November.

---

### 1.5 Juvenile Plumage

**Ducklings (newly hatched):**
- Underside and face: bright yellow.
- Dark streaks running through/by the eyes.
- Back: black/dark brown with four distinct yellow spots.
- Top and back of head: dark.
- Legs and bill: dark grey/black.
- Overall pattern: striking yellow-and-black.

**Growing juveniles (3--10 weeks):**
- Bright yellow fading to drab brown.
- Pin feathers emerge on flanks and rump from ~3 weeks.
- By ~10 weeks, plumage closely resembles the adult female (streaky brown).
- Can be distinguished from adults by: brown outer tail feathers, faded/worn wing feathers, notched juvenal tail feathers.

**Sub-adult males (6--10 months):**
- Gradually acquire adult male colouring over the first autumn/winter.
- Green head colour comes in patchy at first.

---

### 1.6 The Speculum (Wing Patch)

The speculum is one of the most important field marks on a Mallard and is present in **both sexes, year-round**.

- **Colour:** Iridescent purple-blue. The exact hue shifts between blue and purple depending on light angle (structural coloration produced by microscopic hexagonal lattices on feather surfaces).
- **Borders:** Bordered on both leading and trailing edges by prominent **white bars**.
- **Position:** Located on the secondary flight feathers of the wing. Visible at rest (as a patch on the folded wing near the flank) and very prominent in flight.
- **Minecraft note:** This is visible from behind when the bird is in flight. In a model, it should appear as a bright blue-purple rectangle with white edges on each wing.

## 2. Shape and Proportions

### 2.1 Body Shape

- **Heavy, hefty, robust** body -- large for a dabbling duck.
- **Rounded head** with a smooth, domed profile.
- **Short, thick neck.**
- **Long body** relative to overall size.
- **Stout, rounded build** -- gives an impression of bulk and solidity.

### 2.2 Bill Shape

- **Broad, flat, spatulate** -- the classic "duck bill" shape.
- Wide and flattened, with lamellae (comb-like structures) along the edges used for filtering food from water.
- Slightly wider at the tip than at the base.
- Has a hard nail at the very tip used for gripping food.

### 2.3 Postures

**On water:**
- Sits high on the water (typical of dabbling ducks, unlike diving ducks which ride low).
- Gentle slope from rounded chest down to the tail.
- Tail often slightly cocked upward, giving a blunt rear profile.
- Overall silhouette: long body, high-riding, tail up.

**On land:**
- Waddles with a distinctive side-to-side gait (legs are set far apart and far back on the body).
- Body held roughly horizontal, slightly angled upward at the front.
- Comfortable and confident on land -- walks, rests, and feeds in fields and parks.

**In flight:**
- Long body, relatively short neck extended forward.
- Wings appear relatively narrow and pointed for body size.
- Rapid, stiff wingbeats.
- Feet trail behind, visible past the tail.
- White underwing linings contrast with darker body.
- Speculum visible as a bright flash on each wing.

## 3. Habitat and Biome

### 3.1 Preferred Habitats

Mallards are extreme habitat generalists. They use virtually any shallow freshwater (and some brackish) wetland:

- **Ponds and lakes** (including small farm ponds and large reservoirs)
- **Rivers and streams** (especially slow-moving stretches)
- **Marshes, bogs, and swamps**
- **Flooded meadows and floodplains**
- **Estuaries and coastal lagoons** (brackish water)
- **Park lakes and ornamental ponds** (very common in urban settings)
- **Rice paddies and flooded agricultural fields**
- **Beaver ponds**
- **Prairie potholes** (important for North American breeding populations)
- **Roadside ditches**

**Key requirement:** Shallow water (less than 0.9 m / 3 ft preferred) where they can dabble and upend to reach the bottom.

**Should NOT spawn in:** Desert, badlands, deep ocean, mountains above treeline, the Nether, the End.

### 3.3 Urban Presence

Mallards are among the most urban-adapted wild birds:
- Extremely common in city parks, canals, and ornamental lakes.
- Readily approach and accept food from humans.
- Nest in gardens, window boxes, flowerpots, rooftops, and balconies -- even high-rise buildings (a 9th-floor apartment balcony in Manchester, England is documented).
- Often the only wild duck many city-dwellers encounter.
- **Minecraft implication:** Could spawn near villages or other player-built structures near water.

### 3.4 Seasonal Movements

- **UK:** Resident breeding population (~61,000 pairs). Winter numbers swell to ~675,000 birds with arrivals from Iceland, Scandinavia, Russia, the Netherlands, France, and the Baltic States.
- **North America:** Northern breeders migrate south in autumn; southern populations may be sedentary.
- **General pattern:** Most northern populations are migratory; temperate populations are largely sedentary.
- Congregate in large flocks on open water in winter.

## 4. Diet and Feeding

### 4.1 Primary Food Sources

Mallards are **omnivorous generalist feeders**. The majority of the diet is plant material.

**Plant foods:**
- Seeds of sedges, grasses, pondweeds, smartweeds, bulrushes
- Stems and roots of aquatic vegetation
- Duckweed and other floating plants
- Acorns and other tree seeds
- Waste grain (wheat, barley, corn, rice)
- Aquatic algae

**Animal foods:**
- Aquatic insect larvae (midges, dragonflies, flies)
- Snails and slugs
- Freshwater shrimp and other crustaceans
- Earthworms
- Small fish (occasionally)
- Fish eggs
- Tadpoles

### 4.2 Dabbling Behaviour

Mallards feed primarily by **dabbling** -- a set of surface and shallow-water feeding techniques:

- **Surface skimming:** Bill held flat on water surface, filtering food through lamellae.
- **Upending / tipping:** Head plunges below the surface, tail points straight up in the air. Reaches food on the bottom in shallow water. This is the iconic "bottoms up" posture.
- **Head dipping:** Submerges just the head and neck while body remains on the surface.
- **Occasional diving:** While primarily surface feeders, Mallards *can* dive in shallow water (typically less than 1--2 m). They use alternate paddling underwater, unlike true diving ducks which use simultaneous foot strokes. Ducklings dive more readily than adults.

### 4.3 Grazing on Land

- Walk through fields and grassy areas picking at seeds, grain, and vegetation.
- Probe in mud and soil for invertebrates.
- Graze on short grass.
- Forage in agricultural fields for spilled or standing grain.

### 4.4 Urban vs. Wild Diet

| Setting | Primary Diet |
|---|---|
| **Wild -- summer** | Aquatic invertebrates (insects, larvae, snails, shrimp), aquatic plants, seeds |
| **Wild -- winter** | Seeds, grain, roots, tubers, some invertebrates |
| **Wild -- migration** | Largely agricultural grain and seeds |
| **Urban parks** | Human-provided food (bread, seed mixes), supplemented by natural invertebrates, duckweed, grass. Urban Mallards in winter may rely **entirely** on human-provided food. |

**Note:** Bread is not a healthy food for Mallards and should not be encouraged.

### 4.5 Seasonal Diet Changes

- **Spring/Summer (breeding):** Higher proportion of protein-rich animal food -- insects, larvae, snails, shrimp. Females especially need protein for egg production.
- **Autumn/Winter:** Shifts heavily toward plant material -- seeds, grain, roots. Waste grain in harvested fields becomes important.
- **Migration:** Dominated by high-energy seeds and agricultural grain.

## 5. Flight Pattern

### 5.1 Take-off Style

- Mallards can **spring directly upward from the water** -- a near-vertical take-off, sometimes described as "helicopter-like."
- This is a defining characteristic of dabbling ducks (as opposed to diving ducks, which must run along the water surface to build speed).
- Can also take off from land.
- **Minecraft animation note:** The take-off should show the bird launching almost vertically with rapid wingbeats before transitioning to forward flight.

### 5.2 Flight Style

- **Direct and strong** -- flies in a straight line, not undulating.
- **Rapid, stiff wingbeats** -- relatively fast and continuous flapping; does not soar or glide extensively.
- Typical cruising speed: ~80 km/h (50 mph).
- Exceptional recorded speed: 103 mph (166 km/h) with tailwind during migration.
- Wings appear relatively narrow and pointed in flight.
- White underwing linings flash visibly.

### 5.3 Wing Sound in Flight

- Wings produce a characteristic **faint whistling sound** in flight.
- Caused by the slightly curved outer primary feathers catching the air during wingbeats, creating a vibrating noise.
- Loudest when flying fast in a flock.
- Males produce a more noticeable whistle than females (due to more curved wing feathers).
- **Minecraft audio note:** A soft, rhythmic whistling or whirring sound accompanying flight would be authentic.

### 5.4 Landing on Water

- Comes in with feet extended forward, acting as water brakes.
- Lands with a short skid/splash on the water surface.
- More precise and shorter landing run than diving ducks.
- Often makes a slight "plop" sound.

### 5.5 Formation Flying

- Mallards can fly in loose **V-formations** or diagonal lines during migration, similar to geese.
- Birds behind synchronize their wingbeats with the bird in front to take advantage of upwash from wingtip vortices.
- Local flights (not migration) are usually in loose, informal groups rather than strict formations.

## 6. Nesting Behaviour

### 6.1 Nest Site Preferences

- **Primary:** On the ground, in a concealed location near water (within dense vegetation -- tall grass, shrubs, reeds).
- **Distance from water:** Usually close, but can be more than 1.6 km (1 mile) from the nearest water.
- **Elevated sites (occasional):** Tree hollows, old crow nests, haystacks, nest baskets placed above water. Recorded nesting up to 25 feet above ground in a tree crotch.
- **Urban sites:** Boathouses, wood piles, roof gardens, enclosed courtyards, large flowerpots, window boxes, balconies (including high-rise buildings).
- **Cover preference:** Prefers sites with overhead or surrounding vegetation concealment.

### 6.2 Nest Construction

1. Female selects the site and creates a **shallow depression (scrape)** in the ground or substrate.
2. While sitting in the depression, she **pulls surrounding vegetation** toward her (grasses, leaves, reeds) to build up a rim.
3. After incubation begins, she **plucks soft down feathers from her own breast** to line the nest and create insulation.
4. Down is also used to **cover the eggs** when she leaves the nest to feed, helping conceal them and retain warmth.
5. Finished nest: ~30 cm (1 foot) in diameter, bowl-shaped, well-insulated with down.

### 6.3 Breeding Season

- **Egg laying:** Mid-March to end of July (UK). Peaks April--May.
- **Pair formation:** Begins in autumn (October--November); pairs are usually formed by midwinter.
- Mallards are generally **seasonally monogamous** -- pairs stay together for one breeding season but typically do not re-pair the following year.

### 6.4 Clutch Size and Incubation

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| **Clutch size** | 8--13 eggs (typically ~12) |
| **Egg colour** | Creamy white to greenish-buff, unmarked (no speckles) |
| **Laying rate** | One egg every 1--2 days |
| **Incubation start** | Does NOT begin until the clutch is complete (ensures synchronous hatching) |
| **Incubation period** | 27--28 days (range: 25--29 days) |
| **Incubator** | Female only. Male departs after incubation begins. |
| **Nest attendance** | Female sits most of the day, leaving for ~1 hour each morning and afternoon to feed. Covers eggs with down when absent. |

### 6.5 Duckling Behaviour

- **Precocial:** Ducklings hatch fully covered in down, eyes open, capable of walking, swimming, and feeding almost immediately.
- **Drying time:** ~12 hours after hatching.
- **Nest departure:** Usually the morning after hatching (13--16 hours old). Less than 24 hours after the last egg hatches, the female leads the brood to water.
- **Swimming:** Can swim from hatching. Follow the mother in a line or tight group on the water.
- **Feeding:** Begin feeding on invertebrates at or near the water surface immediately upon reaching water. Initially catch prey at the surface; later dabble and upend like adults.
- **Imprinting:** Strong filial imprinting -- ducklings instinctively follow the mother and learn habitat, foraging behaviour, and food sources from her.
- **Fledging:** 50--60 days after hatching.
- **Independence:** After fledging, young become independent of the mother.
- **Minecraft note:** Ducklings following the mother in a line on water would be an iconic and accurate behaviour to implement.

## 7. Song and Calls

### 7.1 Female Quack

- The **quintessential duck quack** -- the sound most people think of when they think "duck."
- A loud, resonant **"QUACK"** -- the classic duck call.
- Often given in a **descending series of 2--10 quacks** (called the "decrescendo call" or "hail call").
- First quack is the loudest and highest-pitched; subsequent quacks decrease in volume, pitch range, and duration.
- Used as a long-distance contact call and to draw other ducks.
- Also gives a **paired quack** during courtship.
- Additional calls: maternal calls to coordinate with brood, alarm calls (sharper, more rapid quacking).

### 7.2 Male Call

- Males do **NOT** give the classic quack.
- Instead, the drake gives a **quiet, nasal, rasping "raehb"** -- a one- or two-noted call.
- Sometimes described as a soft, reedy "rab" or "raeb."
- Used to attract females and to alert other Mallards to predators.
- Much quieter and less carrying than the female's quack.
- Males also produce a **mechanical rattling noise** by rubbing the bill against the flight feathers during courtship displays (a form of stylised preening).

### 7.3 Other Vocalisations

- **Whistle (male courtship):** A sharp whistle given during the grunt-whistle display.
- **Grunt (male courtship):** A low grunt following the whistle in the grunt-whistle display.
- **Alarm calls (both sexes):** Sharper, faster versions of their normal calls.
- **Brood calls (female):** Soft, low clucking sounds used to keep ducklings together.
- **Persistent quacking (female):** Used to lead ducklings from nest to water.

## 8. Behaviour

### 8.1 Swimming and Diving

- Swims buoyantly, sitting high on the water with tail slightly raised.
- Propels itself with alternate paddling of webbed feet.
- Head may bob slightly forward and back with each paddle stroke.
- Primarily a surface feeder; rarely dives (but is physically capable of shallow dives).
- When diving, uses alternate foot strokes (unlike simultaneous strokes of true diving ducks).
- Frequently preens while floating -- dips head and rubs oil from preen gland over feathers for waterproofing.
- Sleeps on water (often with head tucked under wing) or on banks.

### 8.2 Social Behaviour

- **Highly gregarious** outside the breeding season.
- Forms large flocks (called a "sord" or "sorde") in winter -- can number in the hundreds or thousands.
- Mixed-sex flocks in winter; males separate from females after incubation begins in spring.
- Tolerant of other duck species and often found in mixed-species flocks.
- **Minecraft note:** Should be found in small groups (3--8 birds) during breeding season; larger flocks (10--30+) in autumn/winter.

### 8.3 Courtship Displays

Mallard courtship is elaborate, involving several named displays, typically performed by groups of males around one or more females:

| Display | Description |
|---|---|
| **Head-Pumping** | Male and female rhythmically bob their heads up and down. Often a precursor to mating. |
| **Grunt-Whistle** | Male rises from the water, arches neck, raises head high, gives a sharp whistle followed by a grunt as he settles back. Takes about 1 second. |
| **Head-Up-Tail-Up** | Male pulls wings and tail up simultaneously with a loud whistle, briefly showing off the purple-blue speculum. A quick, showy gesture. |
| **Down-Up** | Male dips bill into water, then flicks head and body upward, spraying water. |
| **Head-Flick** | Quick sideways flick of the head. |
| **Head-Shake** | Rapid side-to-side shaking of the head on the water surface. |
| **Nod-Swimming** | Male (or female) swims rapidly for a short distance with neck held very low, bill just above the water surface. A form of solicitation or indication of interest. |
| **Bill-Dip** | Bill dipped into water during display sequence. |
| **Bill-to-Feather (Stylised Preening)** | Male rubs bill against flight feathers, producing a rattling sound. Shows off the speculum to the female. |

### 8.4 Territorial Behaviour During Breeding

- Males become more aggressive during the breeding season.
- Drakes will chase rival males away from their mate.
- Aggressive displays include head-low charging across the water, pecking, and wing-flapping.
- "Rape flights" -- multiple drakes may pursue and force copulation on unwilling females; this is a well-documented but controversial behaviour.
- Males do not defend a fixed territory; instead, they defend their mate (mate-guarding).
- After the female begins incubation, the male abandons the pair bond and may join bachelor groups.

### 8.5 Moulting and Flightless Period

- **Timing:** June--August (after breeding).
- Males moult first into eclipse plumage; females moult after the ducklings are independent.
- **Wing moult:** All flight feathers (remiges) are shed simultaneously over just a few days, rendering the bird **completely flightless for 3--4 weeks**.
- During the flightless period, Mallards retreat to dense marshes, reedbeds, or the centres of large lakes for protection from predators.
- Body feathers are replaced concurrently with the wing moult (males) or shortly after (females).
- By September--October, males regain flight capability and begin moulting back into breeding plumage.
- **Minecraft note:** A possible seasonal mechanic -- Mallards in summer could temporarily lose the ability to fly, retreat to water, and display duller plumage.

### 8.6 Interaction with Humans (Park Ducks)

- The Mallard is the archetypal "park duck" -- extremely habituated to humans in urban environments.
- Approaches people closely, especially if food is expected.
- Has been fed bread, grain, peas, and other human food in parks for centuries.
- Remarkably tame in urban settings; wild Mallards are considerably more wary.
- Nests in close proximity to human activity (gardens, flower pots, rooftop gardens, balconies).
- One of the few wild birds that many urban children learn to recognise.
- **Minecraft note:** Could be implemented as a mob that does not flee from players (or flees less readily than other wild birds), especially if spawned near villages. Could follow players holding seeds/wheat.
