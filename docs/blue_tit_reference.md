# Blue Tit (Cyanistes caeruleus) - Detailed Reference for Minecraft Mod

---

## Physical Appearance

### Body Dimensions

| Measurement | Value |
|---|---|
| **Length** | 10.5-12 cm (4.1-4.7 in) |
| **Wingspan** | 17.5-20 cm (6.9-7.9 in) |
| **Weight** | ~11 g (0.39 oz), range 9-13 g |

One of the smallest European tits, distinctly smaller than the Great Tit (14 cm).

### Detailed Plumage Description (Adult)

**Head:**
- **Crown:** Bright cobalt-blue cap, bordered by a thin white line that runs across the forehead, over the supercilium, and around the back of the nape, forming a white ring surrounding the blue crown.
- **Forehead and supercilium:** White band above the bill and over the eyes.
- **Cheeks / ear-coverts:** Clean white.
- **Eye stripe:** Dark navy-blue to black postocular stripe running from the base of the bill through the eye to the nape, separating the white supercilium from the white cheeks.
- **Chin stripe:** Black bib/chin patch extending narrowly down the throat; forms a thin dark collar connecting to the nape stripe.
- **Nape:** Dark blue-black line connecting the two eye stripes across the back of the head, with a small white triangle visible at the nape junction.

**Upperparts:**
- **Mantle and back:** Yellow-green to olive-green, sometimes with a slight bluish tinge.
- **Rump:** Blue-green, slightly brighter than the back.

**Wings:**
- **Coverts and flight feathers:** Blue (cobalt to cerulean), with a single prominent white wingbar across the greater coverts.
- **Tertials and secondaries:** Dark grey-blue with pale edges.
- **Primary feathers:** Darker blue-grey with narrow pale fringes.

**Tail:**
- Blue, moderately short, slightly notched.

**Underparts:**
- **Breast and belly:** Sulphur-yellow, with a thin dark central stripe running down the centre of the belly (more prominent in males).
- **Flanks:** Yellow, slightly washed olive.
- **Undertail coverts:** Whitish-yellow.

**Bare Parts:**
- **Bill:** Small, short, and conical; black (upper mandible) with dark grey lower mandible.
- **Legs and feet:** Blue-grey (sometimes described as slate-grey).
- **Eyes (iris):** Dark brown to black.

### Sexual Dimorphism

Sexual dimorphism is subtle and essentially indistinguishable to the human eye under normal light:

- **Males:** Slightly brighter, more saturated blue on crown and wings. The blue crown reflects strongly in **ultraviolet light** (invisible to humans but visible to birds). Males have a slightly wider, more defined dark neckband and a more prominent dark central belly stripe. Crown patch tends to be slightly larger.
- **Females:** Marginally duller blue tones, slightly narrower neckband, and a less prominent belly stripe. The UV reflectance of the crown is measurably lower.
- There is no significant difference in bill, leg, or eye colour between sexes.

**For the Minecraft mod:** Given the subtlety, a single model for both sexes is reasonable. If dimorphism is desired, make the male's blue crown slightly brighter/more saturated and the belly stripe slightly more visible.

### Juvenile Plumage

- **Crown:** Greenish-yellow to dull olive-green (lacks the bright blue of adults).
- **Face:** Yellow-washed cheeks (not the clean white of adults); eye stripe is less defined and browner.
- **Upperparts:** Duller olive-green.
- **Wings:** Less vivid blue, with a less prominent wingbar.
- **Underparts:** Paler, more washed-out yellow.
- Overall appearance: Softer, less contrasting, with a distinctly "washed-out" or muted version of adult plumage.
- Juveniles undergo a **partial post-juvenile moult** in late summer/early autumn, replacing body feathers (but not wing and tail feathers) with adult-type plumage.

### Seasonal Plumage Changes

- Blue Tits undergo a **single complete annual moult** in late summer (July-September), shortly after breeding.
- **Fresh autumn plumage:** Colours are at their most vivid immediately after moult; the blue crown and wings are bright, and the yellow underparts are richest.
- **Spring/breeding plumage:** Feather tips wear away over winter, often exposing slightly brighter underlying colour. Crown colour peaks around the breeding season, then decreases sharply afterwards.
- Males begin moult before females. Birds with smaller broods moult earlier and faster.
- During active moult, birds can look notably scruffy with patchy, uneven plumage.

## Shape and Proportions

- **Body shape:** Small, round, and compact -- almost spherical when fluffed up in cold weather.
- **Head:** Relatively large for body size, giving a "big-headed" appearance.
- **Bill:** Very small, short, fine, and slightly conical. Notably small relative to head size.
- **Tail:** Moderately short, slightly notched.
- **Legs:** Short, well-suited for clinging and perching on thin branches.
- **Overall impression:** Dumpy, energetic, plump little bird. Often described as "rotund."

### Typical Postures

- **Acrobatic clinging:** Frequently clings to the underside of branches, twigs, and leaves. Often hangs completely **upside-down** while foraging -- a signature behaviour.
- **Alert perched posture:** Upright, head slightly tilted, often turning rapidly to scan surroundings.
- **Fluffed roosting posture:** In cold weather, fluffs feathers into a near-perfect sphere to conserve heat.
- **Feeding on ground:** Hops rather than walks, with quick, bouncy movements.

**For the Minecraft mod:** Key animations to consider include: upside-down hanging, sideways clinging to tree trunks/branches, rapid head-turning while perched, and hopping on the ground.

## Habitat and Biome

### Preferred Habitats in Britain

- **Primary habitat:** Deciduous and mixed woodland, especially oak woodland (due to caterpillar abundance on oaks).
- **Secondary habitats:** Hedgerows, parks, gardens (both urban and suburban), orchards, scrubland.
- **Urban presence:** Extremely common in gardens and towns. Present in **98% of all British gardens** surveyed. Well-adapted to urban environments.
- **Rural presence:** Abundant in farmland hedgerows and rural woodlands.
- **Wetland edges:** Will use alder and willow carr in wetland margins.
- **Avoids:** Dense coniferous forests (prefers deciduous), open moorland, treeless habitats.

### Relationship with Bird Feeders and Gardens

- One of the most frequent garden bird feeder visitors in the UK.
- Particularly attracted to peanut feeders, sunflower seed feeders, and fat/suet balls.
- Famous for their ability to cling to hanging feeders that exclude larger birds.
- Historically famous for learning to **open foil milk bottle caps** to drink cream from doorstep-delivered milk bottles -- a behaviour that spread culturally across the UK in the mid-20th century, demonstrating social learning.
- Population may have been boosted by widespread garden bird feeding.

## Diet and Feeding

### Primary Food Sources

**Spring/Summer (breeding season):**
- **Caterpillars** (especially moth caterpillars, particularly winter moth larvae from oak trees) -- the single most important food for nestlings.
- Aphids, flies, lacewings, small beetles, spiders, and other small invertebrates.
- Each chick can eat up to **100 caterpillars per day**; with an average brood of ~10 chicks, parents must deliver around **1,000 caterpillars daily**.
- Adults consume over **400 different types of prey** items during the breeding season.

**Autumn/Winter:**
- Seeds (birch, alder, beech mast).
- Nuts (fragments, especially from feeders).
- Berries and small fruits.
- Peanuts and sunflower seeds from garden feeders.
- Fat and suet from feeders.
- Will occasionally take small scraps.

### Feeding Technique

- **Gleaning:** Picks insects and larvae from leaves, bark crevices, and twigs.
- **Hanging:** Hangs upside-down from the tips of thin branches and twigs to reach food inaccessible to heavier or less agile birds. This is a defining characteristic.
- **Feeder specialist:** Clings to hanging feeders (peanut cages, seed tubes, fat balls) with great ease.
- **Ground feeding:** Hops on the ground to pick up fallen seeds, but prefers elevated feeding positions.
- **Foraging niche:** Preferentially forages on the **outer parts of branches** and in the canopy, as opposed to Great Tits (which use inner branches) and Long-tailed Tits/Goldcrests (which use the very tips/twigs).

### Problem-Solving Intelligence

- Blue Tits are renowned for their intelligence and problem-solving ability.
- In laboratory experiments, they have learned to pull out pegs, open matchbox drawers, and navigate multi-step puzzles to access food.
- The milk bottle cap opening behaviour (1920s-1950s) is one of the most cited examples of cultural transmission/social learning in birds.

## Flight Pattern

### Flight Style

- **Rapid, whirring wingbeats** producing a slightly buzzy quality -- wings beat very fast due to small size.
- **Slightly undulating flight path:** Not as pronounced as woodpeckers or finches, but a gentle rise-and-fall pattern on longer flights.
- Flights are typically **short and direct**, hopping between nearby trees, bushes, or feeders.
- Agile and manoeuvrable in dense vegetation; can navigate through tangled branches with ease.

### Display Flights

During courtship/pair-bonding, males perform several display flights:
- **Moth flight:** Shallow, rapid wingbeats with a slow, fluttering descent (resembling a large moth).
- **Butterfly flight:** Deep, slow wingbeats with exaggerated up-and-down motion.
- **High flight:** Flies up to ~6 metres, then dives back down.

### Movement Through Canopy

- Moves through the canopy with quick hops along branches.
- Frequently flits between nearby twigs with very short flights (1-3 metres).
- Uses acrobatic clinging, hanging, and scrambling rather than sustained flight within trees.

### Ground Movement

- Hops (does not walk) on the ground with quick, bouncy movements.
- Spends relatively little time on the ground compared to Great Tits.

**For the Minecraft mod:** Flight animation should show rapid, buzzy wingbeats with a slight up-and-down undulation. Keep flight distances short -- the bird should prefer hopping between nearby blocks/trees rather than long sustained flights.

## Nesting Behaviour

### Nest Site Preferences

- **Natural cavities:** Holes in trees (especially in deciduous trees like oak, birch, and beech), old woodpecker holes, crevices in walls.
- **Nest boxes:** Readily adopts nest boxes; prefers entrance hole diameter of **25 mm** (which excludes Great Tits, which need 28 mm).
- **Unusual sites:** Occasionally in letterboxes, pipes, lamp posts, or other cavities.
- Sites must have a clear flight path to the entrance and be relatively inaccessible to predators.

### Nest Construction

Built by the **female alone** over approximately 1-2 weeks:
1. **Base layer:** Moss, dried grass, dead leaves -- fills the bottom of the cavity.
2. **Cup structure:** Shaped into a neat cup from the base materials.
3. **Lining:** Soft feathers, hair, wool, fine plant fibres. The feather lining is important for insulation.

### Breeding Timeline

| Stage | Timing |
|---|---|
| **Pair formation** | January - March |
| **Nest site selection** | February - March |
| **Nest building** | Late March - April |
| **Egg laying** | Late April - May (one egg per day, laid early morning) |
| **Incubation** | 12-16 days (typically 13-15); begins when penultimate egg is laid |
| **Hatching** | Mid to late May |
| **Nestling period** | 18-21 days in nest |
| **Fledging** | Early June typically |
| **Post-fledging dependence** | 1-2 weeks after leaving nest |

### Clutch Size

- Blue Tits have one of the **largest clutch sizes of any European bird**: typically **8-12 eggs**, occasionally up to **16**.
- Average is about 10 eggs.
- Eggs are white with fine reddish-brown speckles, approximately 16 x 12 mm.

### Incubation

- **Female only** incubates.
- Male feeds the female on the nest during incubation.
- Duration: 12-16 days (usually 13-15).

### Broods Per Year

- Normally **one brood per year**.
- Will attempt a replacement clutch if the first fails early in the season, but this is uncommon.
- True second broods are rare.

### Chick and Fledgling Survival

- Both parents feed the nestlings.
- Starvation is a significant cause of mortality; **21% of fledglings are found dead within the first month** after leaving the nest.
- Timing of breeding is critical: hatching must coincide with peak caterpillar availability on oak trees (the "caterpillar peak").

## Song and Calls

### Song

- **Structure:** Two or three high-pitched, thin notes followed by a lower-pitched trill/rattle.
- **Phonetic transcription:** "tsee-tsee-tsee-chu-chu-chu" or "si-si-si-hi-hi-hi-hi" or "tsee-tsee chitta-chitta."
- **Character:** Not particularly tuneful compared to some songbirds; bright, thin, and silvery. The trill portion varies in length and speed.
- **Singing period:** Mainly January to June, peaking February-April. Males sing from exposed perches in treetops to establish territory.

### Call Types

| Call Type | Description | Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Contact call** | Thin, high-pitched "tsee" or "tsi," repeated several times | Maintaining flock cohesion; pairs keeping in touch while foraging |
| **Scolding / alarm call** | Harsh, churring "tseee-rrrrrrrt" or chattering rattle | Response to ground predators (cats, foxes), perched owls. Often followed by mobbing behaviour |
| **Aerial predator alarm** | High-pitched, thin whistle ("seee") | Warning of sparrowhawks, buzzards, or other flying predators overhead |
| **Begging call** | High, insistent peeping | Nestlings and fledglings begging for food |
| **Conversational purr** | Low, purring trill, almost constant | General social chatter; heard virtually year-round |

## Behaviour

### Social Behaviour

- **Winter:** Forms **mixed-species foraging flocks** with other tits (Great Tits, Coal Tits, Marsh Tits, Long-tailed Tits) plus Goldcrests, Nuthatches, and Treecreepers. Blue Tits are typically the **most numerous species** in these flocks and frequently lead them.
- **Breeding season:** Pairs break away from winter flocks; pair bonds form as early as January.
- **Social dominance hierarchy** (interspecific): Great Tit > **Blue Tit** > Marsh Tit > Coal Tit > Long-tailed Tit. Blue Tits are subordinate to Great Tits but dominant over smaller tit species.
- Blue Tits tolerate conspecifics (other Blue Tits) at closer distances than they tolerate other species.

### Territorial Behaviour

- Males establish and defend **breeding territories** in late winter/early spring through song.
- Territory defence is primarily vocal (singing from prominent perches) rather than physical.
- Less aggressively territorial in winter when participating in mixed flocks.
- Home ranges in winter overlap extensively with other individuals and species.

### Relationship with Great Tits

- The two species frequently coexist but partition resources:
  - Blue Tits forage on **outer branch tips** and in the upper canopy.
  - Great Tits forage on **inner branches** and more frequently on the ground.
- Blue Tits use **25 mm nest box holes** (Great Tits need 28 mm), which gives them exclusive access to smaller cavities.
- Great Tits are socially dominant and may displace Blue Tits from feeders.
- Despite competition, the two species regularly flock together in winter.

### Activity Patterns

- **Diurnal** (active during daylight).
- Most active in the **early morning** and **late afternoon**.
- Roost in cavities, dense vegetation, or nest boxes overnight.
- Active throughout the year; **non-migratory** in Britain (largely sedentary), though some seasonal movements occur between rural breeding areas and urban gardens in winter.

### Agility and Acrobatics

- Among the most acrobatic of all European birds.
- Regularly hangs **upside-down** from twigs, seed heads, and feeders.
- Can cling to vertical surfaces and feed at extreme angles.
- Moves with rapid, darting energy; rarely stays still for long.
- Their light weight (~11 g) allows them to exploit the thinnest, most flexible branches and twigs that cannot support heavier species.

### Intelligence and Learning

- Demonstrated ability to solve multi-step puzzles in laboratory experiments.
- The historic **milk bottle foil-cap opening** behaviour (spreading culturally across the UK from the 1920s-1950s) is a classic example of avian innovation and social learning.
- 54% of naive individuals can learn novel foraging tasks from demonstrators (social learning).

### Predators

- **Main predators:** Sparrowhawks, domestic cats, Great Spotted Woodpeckers (raid nests), weasels and stoats (enter nest cavities), Jays, Magpies.
- Mobbing behaviour: Blue Tits will join other small birds to mob perched owls and other predators, producing loud scolding calls.

## Conservation Status

- **UK population:** Approximately **3.4 million breeding pairs**.
- **BTO trend:** Long-term increase, with a shallow decline since approximately 2008.
- **IUCN Red List:** Least Concern.
- **UK conservation status:** Green listed (no conservation concern).
- Population has likely benefited from widespread garden feeding and milder winters.
