# Peregrine Falcon (*Falco peregrinus*) - Comprehensive Reference for Minecraft Mod

---

## Physical Appearance

### Body Dimensions

| Measurement | Male (tiercel) | Female (falcon) |
|---|---|---|
| **Body length** | 34-40 cm (13-16 in) | 46-58 cm (18-23 in) |
| **Wingspan** | 74-100 cm (29-39 in) | 100-120 cm (39-47 in) |
| **Weight** | 330-700 g (12-25 oz) | 700-1,500 g (25-53 oz) |
| **Wing chord** | 26.5-31 cm (10.4-12.2 in) | 31-39 cm (12.2-15.4 in) |
| **Tail length** | 13-19 cm (5.1-7.5 in) | 13-19 cm (5.1-7.5 in) |
| **Tarsus** | 4.5-5.6 cm (1.8-2.2 in) | 4.5-5.6 cm (1.8-2.2 in) |

### Sexual Dimorphism

The female is up to 30% larger than the male and can weigh up to 50% more than her breeding mate. This is typical of raptors ("reverse sexual dimorphism"). The male is traditionally called the "tiercel" (meaning one-third, referencing the size difference) and the female is simply the "falcon."

### Detailed Adult Plumage

- **Crown / cap**: Dark slate-black to blue-black, forming a continuous "helmet" that extends from the forehead down past the eye.
- **Moustachial stripe**: Broad, conspicuous black wedge extending downward from below the eye across the cheek. This is the falcon's most distinctive field mark. Contrasts sharply with the white throat and pale cheek.
- **Cheeks / auriculars**: Pale whitish to cream, framed between the dark cap above and the moustachial stripe below.
- **Throat**: Clean white, contrasting strongly with the dark head.
- **Nape**: Blue-grey to slate, continuous with the back colouring.
- **Back and mantle**: Blue-grey to slate-grey (can appear bluish-black in some light), with faint darker barring.
- **Rump**: Blue-grey, slightly paler than the back in some individuals, with fine barring.
- **Tail**: Blue-grey with narrow dark barring and a broad dark subterminal band; narrow white tip. Relatively long compared to other falcons.
- **Upperwing**: Blue-grey to dark slate with darker barring; flight feathers darker towards the tips. Wingtips distinctly black.
- **Underwing**: Whitish to pale grey, heavily barred with dark brown or black bars across the flight feathers and coverts. Pale base contrasts with darker barring, giving a "checked" appearance in flight.
- **Breast**: White to pale cream (sometimes with a warm buff or tawny-rufous wash), finely spotted or lightly barred with dark brown or black. Upper breast is often the palest area, sometimes appearing almost unmarked.
- **Belly**: White to pale tawny-rufous, more heavily barred with thin, clean bands of dark brown or black. Barring is denser and more pronounced than on the breast.
- **Flanks**: Heavily barred dark brown on white/buff, consistent with belly pattern.
- **Undertail coverts**: Pale, barred.
- **Cere**: Bright yellow (variable towards orange); male usually brighter than female; both brightest during breeding season.
- **Bill**: Dark blue-black with a yellow base (cere). Upper mandible features the prominent **tomial tooth** -- a sharp notch/projection unique to falcons, used to sever prey's spinal cord. The lower mandible has a corresponding notch (mandibular notch).
- **Eye colour**: Dark brown (appears nearly black at distance).
- **Eye-ring (orbital ring)**: Bare skin, bright yellow in adults (matching the cere).
- **Legs and feet**: Bright yellow with large, powerful toes and sharp black talons. Feet are disproportionately large relative to body size.
- **Talons**: Black at all ages (pale only at hatching).

### Juvenile Plumage (first 10-12 months)

- **Upperparts**: Dark chocolate-brown to pale brown (rather than the adult's blue-grey). Feathers may fade to lighter grey-brown.
- **Underparts**: Buff to tawny-cream with bold, dark **vertical streaks** (not horizontal barring as in adults). Streaking is heaviest on the breast and belly.
- **Head**: Dark brown cap; moustachial stripe is present but often thinner and less sharply defined than in adults. Cheeks may be buffer.
- **Cere and orbital ring**: Bluish-white to pale greenish or bluish-grey (not yellow).
- **Feet**: Bluish-grey to bluish-green (not yellow).
- **Eye colour**: Dark brown (same as adult).
- **Overall impression**: Browner, more "messy" looking, with streaked (not barred) underparts and duller bare parts.

### Immature / Subadult Plumage (transitional, ~10-18 months)

- A mix of brown juvenile feathers and incoming blue-grey adult feathers, creating a patchy appearance.
- Head moults first, followed by upperparts and then underparts.
- Cere and orbital ring gradually transition from bluish to yellow.
- Streaked underparts gradually replaced by barred feathers.
- By the second year, most birds closely resemble adults.

## Shape and Proportions

### Body Shape
- **Compact, powerful, broad-chested** with a muscular build. The chest is deep and well-muscled for powering the wings.
- **Torpedo-shaped / streamlined**: The body tapers from broad shoulders to a relatively narrow tail, optimised for aerodynamic efficiency.
- Overall body contour is described as **V-shaped** -- broad at the shoulders, tapering to the tail tip.

### Wing Shape
- **Long, pointed, high-aspect-ratio wings** -- narrow relative to their length, with sharply pointed tips.
- In active flight, the wing profile appears **sickle-shaped** or **scythe-like**.
- In soaring/gliding, wings are held flatter and appear more broadly triangular.
- In the stoop, wings are **completely folded against the body**, creating a teardrop/bullet shape.
- From below in flight, the silhouette can appear **anchor-shaped** -- with the long pointed wings swept back and the tail forming the anchor's shank.

### Relative Proportions
- Head appears relatively small compared to the broad chest.
- Tail is medium-long (roughly equal to or slightly shorter than the wing width).
- Feet and talons are proportionally very large -- adapted for striking prey at speed.
- Bill appears short but deep and powerful.

### Typical Postures
- **Perched**: Sits very **upright and alert**, often on prominent exposed perches (cliff edges, building ledges, pylons). Posture is bolt-upright with the head held high. Wings do not fully extend past the tail tip when folded.
- **In flight**: Wings held relatively flat or slightly bowed downward. In active flight, wingbeats are stiff, shallow, and powerful. In a glide, the wings are swept back slightly.
- **Stooping**: Body compressed into a near-vertical teardrop, wings tucked tight, feet tucked up.

## Habitat and Biome

### Preferred Habitats in Britain
- **Sea cliffs and coastal crags**: Traditional stronghold, especially in Wales, southwest England, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.
- **Inland cliff faces and quarries**: Upland areas of northern England, Scotland, and Wales.
- **Urban environments**: Increasingly common since the 1990s. Tall buildings, cathedrals, power station chimneys, and bridges serve as surrogate cliff faces. Confirmed breeding in London, Derby, Sheffield, Birmingham, Exeter, Bath, Brighton, Norwich, Cambridge, Nottingham, Southampton, Winchester, Edinburgh, Aberdeen, and many more.
- **Open country**: Moorland, farmland, and estuaries (particularly in winter for hunting).
- **River valleys and gorges**: Where suitable rock faces exist.

### UK Population
- Estimated at approximately 1,769 breeding pairs (2014 BTO/RSPB survey).
- Major distributional shift over recent decades: away from uplands and towards lowland regions and the coast. English pairs have increased fivefold since 1981 and now outnumber Scottish pairs.
- Historical population crash in the 1950s-60s due to organochlorine pesticides (DDT); recovery since banning.

### Territory Size
- Urban territories can be remarkably compact: in London, mean nearest-neighbour distance between pairs is approximately **2.16 km**.
- In more natural settings, spacing is wider: e.g., San Francisco Bay ~7.69 km, Cape Peninsula ~3.13 km.
- Rural/upland territories in the UK are typically several kilometres across.

### Appropriate Minecraft Biomes
- **Stony Peaks / Stony Shore**: Closest equivalent to cliff-nesting habitat.
- **Windswept Hills / Windswept Gravelly Hills**: Represents upland/moorland habitat.
- **Plains / Sunflower Plains**: Open country for hunting.
- **Any biome with villages or structures**: Represents urban adaptation -- could spawn on or near tall structures.
- **Beach / Coast**: Coastal cliffs.
- **River**: River valley habitats.
- **Taiga / Old Growth Taiga**: Northern, wilder habitats (peregrines nest across subarctic regions globally).

## Diet and Feeding

### Primary Prey Species (UK)
- **Feral pigeon** (*Columba livia*): Dominant prey -- 42% by frequency, 63% by biomass in urban studies.
- **Common starling** (*Sturnus vulgaris*): 9% by frequency.
- **Common swift** (*Apus apus*): Important summer prey, ~9% of diet.
- **Collared dove** (*Streptopelia decaocto*): ~4% by frequency.
- **Redwing** (*Turdus iliacus*): ~4% (winter migrant prey, often hunted at night under artificial light).
- **Greenfinch** (*Chloris chloris*): ~4%.
- **Eurasian teal** (*Anas crecca*): ~2% by frequency, 4% by weight.
- **Blackbird** (*Turdus merula*): Regular prey.
- Also takes **waders, gulls, ducks, fieldfares, woodcocks** in winter, especially around estuaries.
- Globally, over **450 bird species** documented as prey, plus bats and occasionally small mammals.
- Prey range: from hummingbird-sized up to sandhill crane-sized birds (though medium-sized birds are the norm).

### Hunting Techniques

#### The Stoop (High-Speed Dive)
- The peregrine's signature hunting method.
- Begins from a soaring position **300-3,000 feet (90-900 m)** above the target.
- The falcon folds its wings tight against its body, forming a streamlined teardrop shape.
- **Maximum recorded speed: approximately 389 km/h (242 mph)** in controlled conditions (National Geographic study). Reliably radar-measured speeds exceed **320 km/h (200 mph)**. Some conservative estimates cite highest confirmed speeds around **184 km/h (114 mph)** from older radar tracking.
- A bony tubercle in each nostril acts as a baffle, slowing airflow and allowing the bird to breathe at extreme speed.
- A third eyelid (nictitating membrane) protects the eyes from debris and drying.

#### Strike Method
- At the bottom of the stoop, the falcon **clenches one foot into a fist** and punches the prey, stunning or killing it with the impact force.
- Alternatively, it may grab the prey directly with open talons.
- After striking, the falcon loops back to catch the tumbling prey in mid-air.

#### Level Pursuit
- Sometimes hunts in fast, low-level powered flight, pursuing prey horizontally.
- Less dramatic than the stoop but effective against prey flushed from cover.

#### Ambush from Perch
- Watches from a high vantage point and launches when prey passes below.

### Post-Capture Prey Handling
1. **Killing bite**: The falcon uses its **tomial tooth** (the notch in the upper bill, matching a groove in the lower bill) to bite through the cervical vertebrae, severing the spinal cord. This is the definitive killing method unique to falcons.
2. **Plucking**: Prey is taken to a regular "plucking post" (cliff ledge, building parapet, tree branch, or ground) where feathers are methodically plucked before consumption. Plucking sites accumulate large quantities of feathers and can be used to identify which species are being eaten.
3. If prey is too heavy to carry, the falcon drops to the ground to pluck and eat it in situ.
4. Small prey may be dismembered and eaten on the wing.

### Hunting Success Rate
- Approximately **20% success rate** overall, though this varies with conditions and prey type.

## Flight Pattern

### Normal Cruising Flight
- **Stiff, shallow, powerful wingbeats** interspersed with short glides.
- Cruising speed: approximately **40-60 mph (65-100 km/h)**.
- Wingbeats are quick and snappy, not deep or languid.
- Flight profile appears direct and purposeful.

### Soaring
- Holds wings relatively flat or slightly bowed, with spread tail.
- Circles on thermals and updrafts along cliff faces.
- Can soar for extended periods when searching for prey or during display flights.
- Wing profile appears broader and more rounded when fully spread for soaring.

### The Stoop (Detailed Mechanics)
1. **Climb phase**: Gains altitude by soaring or powered climbing flight, often to 300-3,000 ft.
2. **Target acquisition**: Spots prey below, often from great distance (exceptional eyesight -- estimated 8x human visual acuity).
3. **Entry**: Tucks wings partially, tips body forward, begins steep descent.
4. **Full stoop**: Wings folded completely against the body. Tail pulled in. Feet tucked close. Body forms a compact, elongated teardrop/bullet shape. Spirals or adjusts course with minute wing and tail adjustments.
5. **Terminal phase**: At the last moment, extends feet forward -- either clenched (to punch) or open (to grab).
6. **Pull-out**: After the strike, executes a sharp pull-up, banking steeply to relocate falling prey. The aerodynamic forces during pull-out can exceed **25 G**.

### Wing Shapes at Different Flight Phases
| Phase | Wing Shape |
|---|---|
| Active flight | Swept-back, sickle-shaped; shallow rapid beats |
| Soaring | Spread wide, rounder profile, tail fanned |
| Gliding | Moderately swept, held flat or slightly bowed |
| Stoop (diving) | Fully folded against body; teardrop silhouette |
| Pre-strike | Wings partially open for control |
| Post-strike pull-up | Wings spread sharply for braking |

### Speed Range
- **Perched**: 0
- **Cruising flight**: 40-60 mph (65-100 km/h)
- **Pursuit flight**: Up to 70 mph (112 km/h)
- **Stoop**: 200+ mph (320+ km/h), maximum recorded ~242 mph (389 km/h)

## Nesting Behaviour

### Nest Site Preferences
- **Does not build a nest.** The peregrine is a "scrape nester" -- it creates a shallow depression (scrape) in available substrate on a ledge.
- **Natural sites**: Cliff ledges, typically on sheer rock faces 50-200 m (165-660 ft) high. Prefers ledges approximately two-thirds of the way up the cliff, with overhead shelter (overhang) and a clear view of the surroundings.
- **Urban sites**: Building ledges, cathedral towers, church steeples, power station chimneys, bridge structures, and purpose-built nest boxes/trays filled with gravel.
- The scrape is created through a ritualised scraping motion by both birds (mainly the male), pushing substrate aside to form a shallow bowl in sand, gravel, soil, or debris.
- No nest material is added -- eggs are laid directly in the scrape.
- Peregrines are highly **site-faithful** and will use the same ledge for many years, sometimes decades or even centuries.

### Breeding Season (UK)
- **Pair bonding and courtship**: January-February.
- **Egg laying**: Late March to mid-April (varies with latitude; earlier in southern England, later in Scotland).
- **Incubation**: Begins with the penultimate or final egg.
- **Chick rearing**: May-June.
- **Fledging**: June-July.

### Clutch and Incubation
- **Clutch size**: Typically **3-4 eggs** (range: 1-5).
- **Egg appearance**: Whitish to buff ground colour, heavily blotched and speckled with red-brown or dark brown markings.
- **Incubation period**: **29-33 days**.
- **Incubation duties**: Mainly the female; male takes over during daytime for feeding breaks. Only the female incubates at night.

### Chick Development and Fledging
- Chicks hatch covered in white down.
- Second down coat is thicker and greyer.
- **Fledging period**: **35-42 days** after hatching.
- Average brood size at fledging: approximately **1.5-2.5 young** (some eggs may be infertile; some chicks may not survive).
- Young remain dependent on parents for **a further 2-6 weeks** after fledging, learning to hunt.
- Sexual maturity reached at **1-3 years** (most breed at 2-3 years).

## Song and Calls

Peregrine Falcons do not "sing." Their vocalisations are harsh, loud calls used in specific behavioural contexts. Four main call types:

### 1. Cack / Kak (Alarm Call)
- **Sound**: Harsh, loud, rapid "kak-kak-kak-kak-kak" or "cack-cack-cack."
- **Structure**: Repeated short notes (~0.15 seconds each), broad-band (1-9 kHz), with moderate inter-call intervals (0.1-0.2 seconds).
- **Context**: Alarm and territorial defence. Given when intruders (humans, other raptors, rival peregrines) approach the nest or territory. Intensity increases with proximity of threat. The most commonly heard call.

### 2. Chitter (Copulation / Excitement Call)
- **Sound**: Rapid, feverish "chi-chi-chi-chi-chi" in bursts of 5-12 repetitions.
- **Structure**: Very short notes (~0.02 seconds), broad-band (0-5 kHz), with virtually no gap between notes.
- **Context**: Most frequently given by the male just before or during copulation. Also given during food transfers and high-excitement interactions.

### 3. Ee-chip / Eechip (Contact / Courtship Call)
- **Sound**: A two-note "ee-chup" or "ee-chip."
- **Context**: Given during ledge displays, courtship, and as a contact call between paired birds. Softer and less aggressive than the cack.

### 4. Wail (Begging / Solicitation Call)
- **Sound**: A drawn-out, rising, plaintive "waaail."
- **Context**: Given by the female to solicit food from the male. Also given by juveniles begging from parents.

## Behaviour

### Territorial Displays
- **Aerial displays**: High soaring flights followed by dramatic stoops near the territory boundary. Male performs repeated steep dives and pull-ups ("undulating flight display").
- **Ledge displays**: Both birds bow to each other at the nest site, accompanied by ee-chip calls.
- **Aggressive defence**: Will attack intruding raptors (including much larger species like buzzards and eagles) with stoops and strikes. Will fight rival peregrines to the death if necessary to defend a nesting territory.
- Crows and ravens rarely mob peregrines (unlike their frequent mobbing of kestrels and sparrowhawks) because the peregrine is capable of killing them.

### Courtship Behaviour
1. **Perching together**: Prospective mates gradually move closer on a shared cliff, perching side by side.
2. **Bowing displays**: Both birds bow repeatedly, calling with ee-chip vocalisations.
3. **Aerial courtship**: The male performs spectacular high-speed aerobatics -- barrel rolls, loops, steep dives, and figure-eights -- often while calling "chup-tuk, chup-tuk."
4. **Prey presentation**: The male catches a bird and presents it to the female, either at a perch or in a dramatic mid-air food pass (the female flips upside-down to receive prey from the male's talons).
5. **Copulation**: Occurs frequently in the weeks before egg-laying, accompanied by the chitter call.

### Activity Patterns
- **Diurnal**: Active during daylight hours. Most hunting occurs in the morning and late afternoon.
- **Crepuscular hunting**: Urban peregrines frequently hunt at dawn and dusk, and even at night under artificial lighting, targeting nocturnal migrants (redwings, fieldfares, woodcocks) attracted to city lights.
- **Loafing / resting**: Spends significant time perched and resting, particularly during the middle of the day. Often sits prominently on exposed perches surveying the surroundings.
- **Sedentary to partially migratory**: UK peregrines are mostly resident year-round, though northern birds may move south in winter. Upland-nesting birds may move to lowland/coastal areas in winter.

### Interaction with Other Raptors
- **Dominant predator**: Apex aerial predator in the UK. No native bird regularly preys on adult peregrines.
- **Kleptoparasitism**: Sometimes steals prey from other raptors.
- **Prey on other raptors**: Known to kill kestrels, merlins, and occasionally sparrowhawks.
- **Avoidance by other species**: Corvids (crows, ravens) are wary of peregrines and rarely mob them, unlike their behaviour towards buzzards, kestrels, and sparrowhawks.
- **Eagle owls**: In continental Europe, eagle owls are a significant predator of peregrines. Not a factor in the UK (though eagle owls are establishing).

### Urban Adaptation
- Tall buildings replicate cliff-face nesting habitat. Peregrines readily adopt purpose-built nest boxes with gravel substrate placed on tall structures.
- Abundant feral pigeon populations in cities provide a reliable year-round food supply.
- Artificial lighting extends hunting hours, allowing nocturnal/crepuscular hunting of migrant birds.
- Urban territories are significantly more compact than rural ones (mean nearest-neighbour distance ~2.16 km in London vs. 7+ km in natural settings).
- Urban peregrines habituate to human activity remarkably well, nesting on busy buildings, cathedrals, and bridges with constant foot traffic below.
- The urban population shift is a major trend in the UK: lowland and urban pairs now outnumber traditional upland pairs.
